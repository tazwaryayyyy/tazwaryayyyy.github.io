1. The Physical Infrastructure Wall

Three separate constraints blocked gigawatt-scale AI infrastructure in 2024-2025, and they have different physical origins with different resolution timelines. Conflating them leads to bad strategic bets about whether an algorithmic substitute is solving the actual problem or a convenient proxy.

HALEU Enrichment Deficit

Advanced reactor designs targeting high energy density require High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium: uranium enriched to 5-20% U-235, compared to the 3-5% in conventional light-water reactors. Both Kairos Power fluoride salt and TerraPower Natrium sodium fast reactor designs depend on it. Total Western LEU enrichment capacity sits at roughly 25.4 million SWU per year: Urenco's four-facility network across the UK, Netherlands, Germany, and New Mexico contributes approximately 17.9 million SWU; Orano's Georges Besse II in Tricastin adds about 7.5 million. Include Japan Nuclear Fuel Limited at Rokkasho and you get another ~1.05 million SWU, pushing the allied total to roughly 26.5 million. None of those facilities produce HALEU at commercial scale. Reconfiguration needs regulatory approval, physical cascade modifications, and new UF6 handling infrastructure rated for higher-enrichment material.

Centrus Energy holds the only US commercial HALEU demonstration license. Their Piketon, Ohio plant runs at approximately 900 kg/year. Centrus has stated a target of 12 metric tonnes per year post-2030. That figure is a 13.3x scale-up from current demonstrated output, contingent on DOE program funding and physical facility expansion. It is a target in a funding proposal, not a licensed production capacity.

The transport problem compounds the enrichment problem. NRC regulations for HALEU shipments reduce cask payload by 44.2% relative to LEU configurations (2,277 kg down to 1,271 kg per cask) because of criticality safety margins at higher enrichment. A reactor with a 5 MT annual fuel requirement ships roughly twice as many casks as an equivalent LEU system. Purpose-designed HALEU transport casks solve this, but no commercial cask design was licensed at the time of writing.

Photonic Wire Bonding Throughput Wall

Co-packaged optics architectures for next-generation AI accelerator interconnects need photonic wire bonding: two-photon polymerization lithography writing 3D polymer waveguide structures between optical components, one bond at a time. Current commercial PWB tools achieve 10-50 bonds per hour in production. Some systems cite an engineered ceiling around 100 bonds per hour. Commercial viability at wafer-scale interconnect density requires each bond in under 30 seconds sustained across thousands of bonds per wafer. No production-grade system is there.

The fundamental constraint isn't write speed. It's alignment tolerance. PWB must hit coupling windows on the order of 1-2 microns between misaligned photonic components. At wafer scale, placement errors accumulate, and every bond requires a fresh alignment search before the write cycle begins. The search-plus-write cycle is the bottleneck, not laser throughput.

The bond material is a cured polymer waveguide with CTE exceeding 50×10⁻&sup6;/K. Silicon runs at 2.6×10⁻&sup6;/K; indium phosphide at approximately 4.6×10⁻&sup6;/K. The interfacial thermal stress across a 125 K excursion (modeling the −40°C to 85°C operating range) is:

Thermal Stress

Simplified 1D (lower bound):

$$\tau_\text{approx} = \Delta\alpha \cdot \Delta T \cdot E_\text{polymer}$$

The biaxial correction adds $E/(1-\nu)$. At polymer Poisson ratio $\nu \approx 0.35\text{--}0.40$, the actual interfacial stress runs roughly 1.54–1.61$\times$ the simplified estimate. Field reliability data from photonic integration pilot programs shows non-trivial failure rates under accelerated thermal cycling. The failure mode is structurally determined by CTE mismatch and doesn't go away through process tuning.

Parallel lithography systems are under development but not commercially deployed. This is a serial-versus-parallel process problem, not a laser power or precision optics problem.

FERC Grid Access and the Co-Location Framework

In November 2024, FERC rejected the amended Interconnection Service Agreement between Talen Energy and Amazon Web Services for a 960 MW nuclear co-location arrangement at Susquehanna. The core objection: the behind-the-meter arrangement let a single customer consume generation without contributing to transmission cost recovery, shifting costs to other PJM ratepayers. That ruling established that co-location needs front-of-meter treatment or equivalent cost recovery. Any analysis treating this as a permanent dead-end stopped reading before 2025.

On June 11, 2025, Talen and Amazon announced a restructured 17-year front-of-meter PPA for up to 1,920 MW through 2042, structured to address the cost-recovery objection. The $18 billion total value circulated in press coverage hasn't been confirmed by primary filings. On December 18, 2025, FERC issued an omnibus order directing PJM to establish new transmission service categories for co-located load configurations. PJM filed its compliance proposal on February 23, 2026, targeting July 31, 2026 as the effective date. As of this writing, the framework is pending final FERC approval. Nuclear co-location isn't dead; it just requires front-of-meter structuring, which adds cost and contract complexity.

EGS Seismicity as a Deployment Blocker

Fervo Energy's commercial EGS projects in Nevada have demonstrated consistent baseload generation at utility scale. Current LCOE runs $90-140/MWh depending on drilling depth, rock permeability, and site conditions; Fervo's reported drilling cost is approximately $4.8 million per doublet. The deployment constraint is geomechanical: fluid injection at pressure changes pore pressure in surrounding rock, which can activate pre-existing faults. The 2017 Pohang, South Korea event (Mw 5.5) resulted in project abandonment after causing structural damage and injuring 135 people. Permitting near populated areas now requires seismic hazard assessments that extend development timelines by 2-4 years. This doesn't scale away with better drilling.

2. The VGCL Thesis

VGCL is a training-time bet: if physical infrastructure constrains compute for the next 3-5 years, get more capability per FLOP by targeting the training distribution at the model's current failure modes rather than sampling uniformly from a static dataset. The loop runs in three phases. Identify categories where the current model fails against a verification oracle. Generate new training problems targeting those categories using a capable external model. Fine-tune, re-evaluate, repeat. The compounding claim is that each round changes the failure distribution, which changes the curriculum, which hits the next failure mode.

The analogy to AlphaGo, AlphaZero, and AlphaProof is structurally apt: all three operate in closed environments with unambiguous feedback. AlphaProof (DeepMind, 2024) extends this to formal mathematics via Lean 4 type-checking. For code generation, the Python interpreter serves the same role. The critical bet is that this loop produces generalization rather than memorization of the curriculum distribution. That requires empirical verification. The five critiques below describe how the current design fails to provide it.

3. The Specification Rift

Structural

The model everyone wants to run and the model you can actually run on a Kaggle T4 are not the same experiment. This isn't just a resource constraint, it changes what any positive result can claim.

Parameter Public Roadmap Practical Spec (Phase 0)
Base model Two identical 7B models Qwen 2.5 1.5B-Instruct
Curriculum size 1,000-5,000 problems 200 problems, 3 epochs
Evaluation HumanEval + MBPP + LiveCodeBench HumanEval + MBPP only
Training infrastructure Dedicated GPU cluster Kaggle T4 (15 GB VRAM)
Curriculum generator Separate, stronger model Claude API (rate-limited)
Oracle Multi-phase verification pipeline Standard Python test suite

The 7B-to-1.5B reduction is hardware-driven. Qwen 2.5 7B-Instruct at fine-tuning batch size 1 with gradient checkpointing requires 16-18 GB VRAM. The 1.5B model fits within 15 GB. The claim that 1.5B is the right model because it has "proportionally less code-specific instruction data" than 3B is a post-hoc rationalization. Both carry the same Instruct fine-tuning pipeline across the family. What actually changes is the per-parameter update magnitude: at fixed learning rate, a 300K token fine-tune perturbs the 1.5B weight configuration more than the 7B one because the gradient update magnitude is constant while the number of parameters being updated is smaller. The token fraction is the same ($300{,}000 / 18 \times 10^{12} \approx 1.67 \times 10^{-8}$ of pre-training volume regardless of size), but the per-parameter perturbation is not. The pre-training gravity argument is real. The model-size reasoning is just the wrong mechanism for it.

A positive result from the practical spec doesn't demonstrate that the roadmap spec works. Curriculum volume, model scale, and oracle sophistication each interact with the training signal in ways that don't scale trivially.

4. The Measurement Instrument Has the Wrong Noise Floor

Statistical

The entire measurement resolution argument is built on the wrong statistical test.

The experiment compares two arms: curriculum-trained vs. control, each evaluated on the same benchmark. The question is whether curriculum training achieves a higher pass rate. That's a two-sample proportion test. The document uses $\sigma_\text{meas}$ throughout, that's the standard deviation of a single evaluation run, which characterizes measurement stability for one arm. The relevant quantity for the comparison is $\text{SE}_\text{diff}$, the standard error of the difference between two independent arm estimates. Those quantities differ by $\sqrt{2}$:

The sqrt(2) Error

Single-arm measurement std (what this document uses):

$$\sigma_\text{meas} = \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}$$

Standard error of the two-arm difference (what the experiment needs):

$$\text{SE}_\text{diff} = \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n} + \frac{p(1-p)}{n}} = \sqrt{2}\;\sigma_\text{meas}$$

The 2$\sigma$ minimum detectable difference is $2 \cdot \text{SE}_\text{diff}$, not $2 \cdot \sigma_\text{meas}$. Every detection threshold in this document is $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.41\times$ smaller than correct.

Running the corrected numbers at $p = 0.5$:

Benchmark ($n$) $\sigma_\text{meas}$ $\text{SE}_\text{diff}$ Corrected 2$\sigma$ threshold
HumanEval (164) 3.90 pp 5.52 pp 11.04 pp
HumanEval + MBPP-raw (538) 2.16 pp 3.05 pp 6.10 pp
HumanEval + MBPP-san (421) 2.44 pp 3.45 pp 6.89 pp
Consequence

The document claims: "at $n=538$, a genuine improvement of 4.31 pp becomes marginally distinguishable from noise." The corrected threshold is 6.10 pp. Statistical power to detect a 5 pp improvement at $n=538$, two-sided $\alpha=0.05$: 37.5%. That's close enough to a coin flip that a null result tells you almost nothing. The experiment at $n=538$ cannot reliably detect the effect it's looking for.

One additional thing to nail down: MBPP (Austin et al., 2021) has three common evaluation variants. The original 374-problem test set gives $n=538$. The 257-problem sanitized subset (which removes ambiguous and low-quality problems, more commonly used in published benchmarks) gives $n=421$ and shifts the 2$\sigma$ threshold to 6.89 pp. The EvalPlus MBPP+ version has 378 problems. The experiment spec must state which split is used before the fact, not after, because the detection threshold depends on it.

The Variance Correction Runs One Direction

The heterogeneous difficulty correction is noted correctly in passing: HumanEval's stratified problem difficulty means the binomial variance is an overestimate, and the proper model is a Poisson-Binomial with per-problem success probabilities $p_i$:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i(1-p_i) \;\leq\; \frac{n}{4}$$

The upper bound is achieved only when all $p_i = 0.5$; with heterogeneous difficulties, variance is lower. That direction of the correction is right. But the variance expression for non-independent outcomes is:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i(1-p_i) \;+\; 2\sum_{i < j} \text{Cov}(X_i,\, X_j)$$

A curriculum engine doesn't improve problems independently. It improves categories. If the model learns to handle recursive data structures, it improves on all recursive data structure problems together, positive within-category correlation. At $m=50$ targeted problems with uniform $p=0.5$ and intra-category correlation $\rho=0.3$, the covariance addition is $\rho \cdot m(m-1) \cdot p(1-p) = 0.3 \times 2{,}450 \times 0.25 = 183.8$, versus an independence contribution of $m \cdot p(1-p) = 12.5$. Effective variance within that block is 196.3 instead of 12.5, a 3.96$\times$ sigma inflation. The net correction direction is unknown without empirical correlation data from this model family on this benchmark. The "upper bound" framing is one-sided.

Also: the expected 2-5 pp curriculum improvement range is load-bearing and unsourced. If the true effect is 10 pp, $n=538$ works fine. If it's 0.5 pp, $n=2{,}000$ is still not enough. That range needs to come from published curriculum fine-tuning results at comparable scale and compute, not from prior expectation.

5. The Adversarial Harness Cannot Distinguish Reasoning from Pattern Extension

Structural

The curriculum generation loop prompts a generator model to write harder problems in category X based on where the current model fails. The generator doesn't have access to the model's internal failure mechanism, it produces problems that are textually similar to the failure category as it appears in its training distribution. If the current model fails at dynamic programming, the generator writes more DP problems. Some of those will genuinely test the missing computation. Some will be solvable by pattern-matching to DP templates the model already knows from pre-training, without requiring the reasoning step that caused the failure.

The Python interpreter cannot distinguish between those two outcomes. Both pass. The oracle doesn't care whether the model understood the algorithm or found a structural template producing correct output on the test cases. This is the Curry-Howard gap: syntactic pattern extension and logical reasoning generalization produce identical behavioral outputs under test-suite evaluation.

What would surface this: the model improves on the specific categories in the curriculum but fails on structurally isomorphic problems in domains not represented in the curriculum, improving on Python DP problems but not equivalent problems in a different syntactic framing. That requires a category transfer check in the evaluation protocol, which isn't in the Phase 0 spec. AlphaZero sidesteps this because self-play generates positions requiring actual policy evaluation; there's no pretraining distribution to pattern-match against. A language model generating code problems doesn't have that property.

6. Phase 3 Oracle Fidelity vs. Simulation Speed

Engineering

For Phase 3 (molecular discovery), the DFT latency critique is right about the constraint and wrong about the failure mode. Yes, raw DFT on drug-sized molecules runs 8-72 hours per query. Running that synchronously inside a live generation loop isn't a viable architecture. But that's not how production materials science ML systems are built.

The standard architecture uses machine learning interatomic potentials (MLIPs): neural networks trained on DFT computations that evaluate molecular energy and forces at approximately $10^5\times$ the speed of the underlying DFT code. CHGNet (Deng et al., 2023) and MatterSim (Microsoft, 2024) are two recent examples. A curriculum loop using an MLIP as the oracle runs at millisecond latency per evaluation, not hours. The DFT latency critique attacks a naive pipeline that no production materials science ML system actually uses.

The real failure mode for Phase 3 is different. Curriculum engines push toward the edges of the current model's capability distribution. In the molecular domain, that means the generator produces candidate structures in regions of chemical space where the model currently fails, which are exactly the regions where MLIPs are least accurate, because MLIPs are trained on DFT data from chemically reasonable structures and extrapolate poorly to unusual geometries, exotic bonding environments, and unexplored coordination chemistries. The curriculum learns to produce structures with low MLIP-predicted energy not because they're genuinely stable and interesting molecules, but because they exploit gaps in the MLIP's coverage. The oracle itself gets adversarially probed by the curriculum generator.

Active Learning as a Partial Response

The standard engineering response to MLIP distributional failure is uncertainty-aware active learning: run the curriculum loop against the MLIP, compute epistemic uncertainty via ensemble disagreement or MC dropout, and route high-uncertainty structures to actual DFT validation rather than trusting the MLIP score. FLARE (Vandermause et al., 2020), MACE-MP-0 (Batatia et al., 2023), and SevenNet (Park et al., 2024) implement this. The uncertainty gate partially addresses oracle gaming by flagging structures in poorly-covered regions before accepting their predicted properties.

But a sufficiently adversarial curriculum generator might find structures in low-uncertainty MLIP regions where the model is confident but systematically wrong, interpolation errors rather than extrapolation errors. Whether that materializes in Phase 3 depends on the specific MLIP's coverage and the generator's capacity. Phase 3 needs to specify: which MLIP version is the oracle, what uncertainty metric triggers DFT fallback, which DFT code handles validation, and how DFT-validated signal flows back to the curriculum generator. Without those four specifications, the oracle architecture is undefined.

7. The Failure Taxonomy Problem

Methodological

The curriculum loop requires categorizing failures to generate a targeted curriculum. Human-assigned problem categories (HumanEval labels by algorithm type: sorting, DP, string manipulation, graph traversal) may not correspond to the model's internal failure modes. A model might fail on graph traversal problems not because it lacks graph reasoning but because it consistently makes off-by-one errors in adjacency list indexing. Both failures appear as "graph traversal" in the human taxonomy but require different curriculum responses.

The inverse is also real. Two problems in different human categories might share a single neural failure mode, failing at pointer arithmetic in C-style simulations and failing at matrix indexing could share a representational deficit in how the model tracks positional state. A curriculum treating them as separate categories generates redundant training signal.

The correct taxonomy is model-architecture-dependent. The same surface category can involve different internal computations in different model families because attention patterns, positional encoding schemes, and MLP specialization differ by architecture. There's no static problem taxonomy that's simultaneously correct for Qwen 2.5, Llama 3, and Mistral. Phase 0 needs either a mechanism for discovering the model-specific failure taxonomy, which is a research contribution in itself, or an explicit acknowledgment that human-assigned categories are a proxy with unknown accuracy.

8. What Actually Needs to Change Before Running Phase 0

Pre-register the statistical test before running anything. The experiment requires a two-sample proportion test with a specific sample size calculation based on desired power and the pre-registered minimum detectable effect. Using $\text{SE}_\text{diff}$:

$\delta$ (true improvement) Power at $n = 538$ Power at $n = 2{,}000$
3 pp 16.6% 47.5%
4 pp 25.9% 71.6%
5 pp 37.5% 88.5%

At $n=2{,}000$ and a genuine 5 pp effect, power is 88.5%. At $n=2{,}000$ and a 3 pp effect, it's 47.5%, still close to a coin flip. The 2-5 pp prior determines whether $n=2{,}000$ is enough. Until that prior is sourced from empirical literature, the required sample size can't be determined. Expanding to 2,000 problems is within reach: HumanEval (164) plus MBPP-raw (374) gives 538; add a fixed-at-experiment-start subset of LiveCodeBench (2024-2025 window, approximately 400 problems) plus HumanEval+ from EvalPlus (164 augmented problems) and you're there. Fix the LiveCodeBench subset before training to prevent contamination from future updates.

Add the category transfer check. Run evaluation on a held-out problem set from a different syntactic domain covering the same algorithmic categories targeted in the curriculum. If the model improves on Python DP in the curriculum but not on equivalent problems in Go or Rust, the improvement is pattern extension. This adds one evaluation call per training round and provides interpretive clarity that no amount of additional benchmark coverage gives you.

Run the LogitLens diagnostic before and after fine-tuning (see Section 10). Check whether probability mass on correct tokens builds from early layers (learning the reasoning computation) or appears only in late layers (surface output adjustment). Two hours of implementation, one forward hook per $N$ layers, separates two outcomes that behavioral evaluation can't separate. It's the highest-leverage addition to the protocol relative to cost.

For Phase 3, specify the oracle architecture before designing the experiment: which MLIP version, what uncertainty threshold triggers DFT validation, which DFT code runs it, how the DFT-validated signal flows back to the curriculum generator. Active learning raises the bar for adversarial exploitation; it doesn't eliminate the failure mode. Make that explicit in the design.

On auto-formalization: for the mathematics domain, converting problem statements to Lean 4 and using the proof assistant's type checker as the oracle partially addresses the Curry-Howard gap. Current LLM-based auto-formalization success rates on simple propositions run 40-60% (wide range, benchmark-dependent, varies significantly by problem complexity and mathlib version). It's an incomplete solution at the edges of formalization coverage, but it provides unambiguous feedback within its scope. More relevant for Phases 2 and 3 than Phase 0, where the Python interpreter already does this.

9. The Test-Time Compute Problem

Independent of VGCL's internal validity issues, there's a competitive framing question. Running pass@100 on HumanEval (164 problems, 100 samples each, approximately 400 tokens per generation) costs roughly $164 \times 100 \times 2 \times 1.5 \times 10^9 \times 400 \approx 2.0 \times 10^{16}$ FLOPs at 1.5B scale. Phase 0 curriculum training over 300K tokens at 3 epochs costs approximately $6 \times 1.5 \times 10^9 \times 9 \times 10^5 \approx 2.7 \times 10^{15}$ FLOPs. The inference run is about 7.3$\times$ the training run. They're within one order of magnitude of each other, not "substantially different budgets."

What actually differs is improvement magnitude. Best-of-100 inference gets roughly 30 pp gain on HumanEval; curriculum training at Phase 0 scale is expected to deliver 2-5 pp. The FLOP efficiency of test-time compute is demonstrably higher at this scale for this benchmark. A model scoring 50% with greedy decoding reaches 80%+ with repeated sampling and test-time verification, the o1 and o3 model series formalize this: inference compute allocated to extended chain-of-thought generation and internal verification steps rather than a single greedy pass.

The stronger case for VGCL isn't efficiency, it's orthogonality. Test-time compute increases the probability of solving a problem the model already knows how to approach given enough tries. Curriculum training changes what the model knows how to approach. On the capability axis they're not substitutes. On the compute budget axis they are, and test-time compute wins at Phase 0 scale. That framing needs to be explicit in any VGCL cost justification.
