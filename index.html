1. Compute Efficiency Thesis vs. Macro-Infrastructure Context

A critical structural error in algorithmic optimization work is the conflation of macro-scale physical infrastructure limits with the localized justification for training-time data selection. The primary argument for Verification-Guided Curriculum Learning does not rest on power-grid limitations or nuclear fuel supply chains. The core claim is scale-independent: at any fixed training compute budget, curriculum sampling efficiency reduces the tokens-to-capability ratio by concentrating gradient updates on high-utility data regimes. This holds equally on a single T4 GPU and on a hyperscale cluster. Physical infrastructure constraints at the gigawatt scale provide macroeconomic motivation for algorithmic efficiency at frontier training runs; they have no direct causal relationship with whether curriculum targeting improves Qwen 2.5 1.5B on HumanEval.

The gigawatt-scale constraints documented below are real, with distinct physical origins and resolution timelines measured in years to decades. They are provided as accurate macro context for the compute landscape, not as the operative justification for Phase 0 experiments.

HALEU Enrichment Deficit

Advanced reactor designs targeting high energy density require High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium: uranium enriched to 5–20% U-235, compared to the 3–5% in conventional light-water reactors. Both Kairos Power fluoride salt and TerraPower Natrium sodium fast reactor designs depend on it. Total Western LEU enrichment capacity sits at roughly 25.4 million SWU per year: Urenco's four-facility network across the UK, Netherlands, Germany, and New Mexico contributes approximately 17.9 million SWU; Orano's Georges Besse II at Tricastin adds about 7.5 million. Japan Nuclear Fuel Limited at Rokkasho contributes another ~1.05 million SWU. None of those facilities produce HALEU at commercial scale; reconfiguration requires regulatory approval, physical cascade modifications, and new UF6 handling infrastructure rated for higher-enrichment material.

Centrus Energy holds the only US commercial HALEU demonstration license. Their Piketon, Ohio plant runs at approximately 900 kg/year. Centrus has stated a target of 12 metric tonnes per year post-2030: a 13.3× scale-up from current demonstrated output, contingent on DOE program funding and physical facility expansion. That figure is a target in a funding proposal, not a licensed production capacity.

The transport problem compounds the enrichment problem. NRC criticality safety margins impose a cask payload reduction that worsens steeply with enrichment level: documented figures show 255 lbs per cask at 12.5% enrichment falling to 54.9 lbs per cask at 100% enrichment, a reduction of approximately 78%, or nearly a fivefold drop in per-shipment payload. The previously circulated figure of 44.2% (2,277 kg to 1,271 kg per cask) substantially underestimates this constraint; it captures only part of the criticality safety margin scaling across the full enrichment range. For reactor-grade HALEU at 19.75% enrichment, the operative per-cask payload falls far below the LEU baseline, and the logistics multiplier on annual shipment volume is correspondingly severe. No commercial HALEU transport cask design was licensed at the time of writing.

Photonic Wire Bonding Throughput Wall

Co-packaged optics architectures for next-generation AI accelerator interconnects require photonic wire bonding: two-photon polymerization lithography writing 3D polymer waveguide structures between optical components, one bond at a time. Current commercial PWB tools achieve 10–50 bonds per hour in production, with an engineered ceiling near 100 bonds per hour. Commercial viability at wafer-scale interconnect density requires each bond completed in under 30 seconds, sustained across thousands of bonds per wafer. No production-grade system is at that throughput.

The fundamental constraint is alignment tolerance, not write speed. PWB must hit coupling windows on the order of 1–2 microns between misaligned photonic components. At wafer scale, placement errors accumulate, and every bond requires a fresh alignment search before the write cycle begins. The search-plus-write cycle is the bottleneck. The bond material is a cured polymer waveguide with CTE exceeding 50×10⁻&sup6;/K; silicon runs at 2.6×10⁻&sup6;/K. The interfacial thermal stress across a 125 K excursion (modeling the −40°C to 85°C operating range) is:

Thermal Stress (Simplified Lower Bound)

$$\tau_\text{approx} = \Delta\alpha \cdot \Delta T \cdot E_\text{polymer}$$

The biaxial correction adds $E/(1-\nu)$. At polymer Poisson ratio $\nu \approx 0.35\text{--}0.40$, actual interfacial stress runs 1.54–1.61$\times$ the simplified estimate. For representative values $\Delta\alpha \approx 10^{-5}$ K$^{-1}$ and $E_\text{polymer} \approx 3$ GPa, a 100 K excursion yields approximately 3 MPa in the simplified form; the biaxial-corrected value reaches approximately 4.6 MPa. Hardware active cooling and strain-absorbing substrates provide limited relief at this stress magnitude. The underlying CTE mismatch is not eliminable through standard process control, but specific software-layer and materials-level interventions can reduce the severity of thermal excursions and provide tolerance for residual misalignment.

Four mitigation vectors are available. First, software workload scheduling can stagger compute tasks across the photonic interconnect fabric, distributing thermal load over time and reducing peak-to-trough temperature variance at any single bond site; the reduced $\Delta T$ amplitude directly lowers the instantaneous stress term. Second, data routing policies can direct traffic away from thermally loaded interconnect regions, avoiding hot-spot layers where bond sites operate near their thermal limit. Third, error-tolerant coding schemes, including redundant photonic signal transmission and forward error-correcting codes applied at the link layer, allow the system to tolerate occasional bond misalignment without propagating hard errors into the compute fabric. Fourth, polymer formulation optimization targeted at reducing $\Delta\alpha$ narrows the stress margin, even where it cannot eliminate it. These interventions do not constitute a resolution of the fundamental alignment constraint; they reduce effective coupling-loss rates under thermal cycling and extend mean-time-to-failure without substituting for the hardware throughput scaling problem.

FERC Grid Access and the Co-Location Framework

In November 2024, FERC rejected the amended Interconnection Service Agreement between Talen Energy and Amazon Web Services for a 960 MW nuclear co-location arrangement at Susquehanna. The core objection: the behind-the-meter arrangement let a single customer consume generation without contributing to transmission cost recovery, shifting costs to other PJM ratepayers. On June 11, 2025, Talen and Amazon announced a restructured 17-year front-of-meter PPA for up to 1,920 MW through 2042, structured to address the cost-recovery objection. On December 18, 2025, FERC issued an omnibus order directing PJM to establish new transmission service categories for co-located load configurations. PJM filed its compliance proposal on February 23, 2026, targeting July 31, 2026 as the effective date. Nuclear co-location is not dead; it requires front-of-meter structuring, which adds cost and contract complexity.

EGS Seismicity as a Deployment Blocker

Fervo Energy's commercial EGS projects in Nevada have demonstrated consistent baseload generation at utility scale. Current LCOE runs $90–140/MWh depending on drilling depth, rock permeability, and site conditions. The deployment constraint is geomechanical: fluid injection at pressure changes pore pressure in surrounding rock, which can activate pre-existing faults. The 2017 Pohang, South Korea event (Mw 5.5) resulted in project abandonment after causing structural damage and injuring 135 people. Permitting near populated areas now requires seismic hazard assessments that extend development timelines by 2–4 years. This constraint does not scale away with better drilling.

2. The VGCL Thesis

VGCL is a training-time optimization: at any fixed compute budget, a curriculum engine targeting the model's current failure modes extracts more capability per FLOP than uniform sampling from a static dataset. The loop runs in three phases. A verification oracle identifies categories where the current model fails. A capable external generator produces new training problems targeting those categories. Fine-tuning, re-evaluation, and iteration continue, with each round updating the failure distribution to expose the next capability gap.

The analogy to AlphaGo, AlphaZero, and AlphaProof is structurally apt: all three operate in closed environments with unambiguous feedback. AlphaProof (DeepMind, 2024) extends this to formal mathematics via Lean 4 type-checking. For code generation, the Python interpreter serves the same oracle role. The critical empirical bet is that this loop produces generalization rather than memorization of the curriculum distribution. The five critiques below describe specific mechanisms by which the current experimental design fails to verify that claim.

Per-Parameter Update Magnitude and Pre-Training Gravity

The attribution of the 1.5B model selection to "proportionally less code-specific instruction data" is incorrect. Both the 1.5B and 7B variants of Qwen 2.5-Instruct carry identical instruction fine-tuning fractions relative to their total pre-training exposure. The structural divergence is governed by per-parameter update dynamics under fine-tuning, not by data composition ratios.

For a transformer with $N$ parameters fine-tuned on $M$ tokens at learning rate $\eta$ with gradient clipping norm $\tau$, under standard Glorot initialization the gradient norm per parameter scales as $\mathcal{O}(d_\text{model}^{-1/2})$ per layer. The expected per-parameter update magnitude for a model with hidden dimension $d_\text{model}$ and $L$ layers is:

$$E[\|\Delta\theta_i\|] \approx \eta \cdot \tau \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{d_\text{model}}} \cdot \frac{1}{L}$$

For the Qwen 2.5 family, the relevant architectural parameters are: 1.5B with $d_\text{model} = 1536$, $L = 28$; 7B with $d_\text{model} = 3584$, $L = 28$. Depth is identical; width diverges. The ratio of expected per-parameter update magnitudes at identical learning rate and clipping threshold:

$$\frac{\|\Delta\theta_{1.5B}\|}{\|\Delta\theta_{7B}\|} = \sqrt{\frac{d_{\text{model},7B}}{d_{\text{model},1.5B}}} = \sqrt{\frac{3584}{1536}} \approx 1.527$$

The 1.5B model absorbs approximately $1.53\times$ larger per-parameter gradient perturbations than the 7B model for identical fine-tuning data at identical learning rate. This variance establishes distinct pre-training gravity regimes. The pre-training gravity radius is defined as the neighborhood in weight space within which pre-trained representations maintain historical structural coherence. This radius is substantially more resilient in the 7B model, where per-parameter updates are diluted across a larger dimensionality. Conversely, the 1.5B model escapes its gravity radius more readily, making it susceptible to either rapid specialization or catastrophic representational decay. The sign of this effect under curriculum training is an empirical question, not a theoretical one. A positive result at 1.5B scale does not predict the direction of the effect at 7B scale, because: (a) the per-parameter perturbation ratio is 1.53×, (b) the pre-training gravity radius may not scale linearly with $d_\text{model}$, and (c) emergent capability thresholds observed at 7B have no established analogs at 1.5B.

QLoRA Feasibility on a Single T4

The 1.5B model selection is not a hardware wall. QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023) with 4-bit NF4 quantization, LoRA adapters, and activation gradient checkpointing places a 7B model well within the 15 GB T4 VRAM envelope:

Component Memory Footprint
NF4 weights (7B × 0.5 bytes) 3.5 GB
LoRA adapters ($r=32$, $\alpha=64$) 0.8 GB
Activations with gradient checkpointing (seq_len=512, batch 1) 3.8 GB
Optimizer states (LoRA params only, AdamW fp32) 0.7 GB
Total ~8.8 GB

Gradient checkpointing adds approximately 33% to backward pass time. The 1.5B model selection is therefore an optimization velocity choice, not a strict technical memory ceiling. The consequence for interpretation is direct: positive Phase 0 results at 1.5B demonstrate that the mechanism operates at that scale, not that it operates at 7B. Curriculum volume, model scale, and oracle sophistication each interact with training signal in ways that do not extrapolate trivially across the 1.53× per-parameter perturbation gap.

NF4 Calibration Boundary Condition and Outlier Channel Remediation

Standard NF4 quantization levels are fixed quantiles of a Gaussian distribution, calibrated by default against text-domain corpora such as C4 or WikiText. Code-domain activation distributions deviate structurally from this assumption: certain channels activate at magnitudes up to 100× the per-layer median, driven by rare tokens or syntax-triggered spikes. These outlier channels lie beyond the learned NF4 clipping range and are clamped to the nearest representable 4-bit level. The consequence is not marginal: an activation that should register at magnitude 100 (in normalized units) is reduced to approximately the NF4 ceiling of ~1.0, injecting structural quantization noise into the weight updates computed by the LoRA adapter for that channel. The LoRA gradients on these channels become effectively random, undermining the curriculum fine-tuning signal on the highest-magnitude features.

Three remediation vectors address this mismatch. First, on-domain recalibration: collect activation histograms from a representative sample of curriculum code problems and recompute the NF4 clip points against the empirical code-domain distribution, rather than importing calibration scales fit to text. This realigns the quantization grid to the actual activation range seen during fine-tuning. Second, group-wise non-uniform quantization: partition channels into magnitude groups and apply a distinct quantization grid per group, so that high-magnitude outlier channels receive a grid matched to their actual range rather than the population-level Gaussian grid. Third, mixed-precision channel isolation: identify channels exceeding a magnitude threshold (typically 3 to 5 standard deviations above the per-layer median) and retain those channels in FP16 throughout the quantization pass, isolating LoRA adapter updates on the critical outlier channels from 4-bit clipping errors entirely. These three vectors are complementary; recalibration reduces the population-level mismatch, group-wise quantization contains residual per-channel variance, and FP16 isolation eliminates clipping on the tail that neither of the prior steps fully resolves.

Additionally, $r = 16$ for a 1536-dimensional model yields $r/d_\text{model} = 0.010$, at the lower boundary of the empirically observed QLoRA curriculum fine-tuning failure zone; increasing to $r = 32$ ($r/d_\text{model} = 0.021$) costs approximately 0.4 GB additional VRAM and substantially enlarges the LoRA subspace available to encode curriculum signal.

4. The Statistical Cascade and Intra-Category Covariance

Statistical

The experiment compares two arms: curriculum-trained versus control, each evaluated on the same benchmark. The operative statistical quantity for this comparison is $\text{SE}_\text{diff}$, the standard error of the difference between two independent arm estimates, not $\sigma_\text{meas}$, the standard deviation of a single-arm evaluation run. These quantities differ by $\sqrt{2}$:

Two-Arm SE Correction

Single-arm measurement standard deviation:

$$\sigma_\text{meas} = \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n}}$$

Standard error of the two-arm difference:

$$\text{SE}_\text{diff} = \sqrt{\frac{p(1-p)}{n} + \frac{p(1-p)}{n}} = \sqrt{2}\;\sigma_\text{meas}$$

The 2$\sigma$ minimum detectable difference is $2 \cdot \text{SE}_\text{diff}$. Every detection threshold computed under the single-arm formula is $\sqrt{2} \approx 1.41\times$ below the correct value.

Running the corrected numbers at $p = 0.5$:

Benchmark ($n$) $\sigma_\text{meas}$ $\text{SE}_\text{diff}$ Corrected 2$\sigma$ threshold
HumanEval (164) 3.90 pp 5.52 pp 11.04 pp
HumanEval + MBPP-raw (538) 2.16 pp 3.05 pp 6.10 pp
HumanEval + MBPP-san (421) 2.44 pp 3.45 pp 6.89 pp
Consequence of the Two-Arm Correction

The corrected 2$\sigma$ detection threshold at $n = 538$ is 6.10 pp, not 4.31 pp. Statistical power to detect a 5 pp improvement at $n = 538$ under a two-sided $\alpha = 0.05$ test is 37.5%: indistinguishable from a coin flip. The experiment at $n = 538$ cannot reliably detect the effect it is looking for.

MBPP (Austin et al., 2021) has three evaluation variants with meaningfully different sample sizes: the original 374-problem test set ($n = 538$ with HumanEval), the 257-problem sanitized subset ($n = 421$, shifting the 2$\sigma$ threshold to 6.89 pp), and the EvalPlus MBPP+ version at 378 problems. The experimental specification must declare which variant is used before training begins; the detection threshold depends on the choice.

Intra-Category Correlation and the Design Effect

Resolving the $\sqrt{2}$ correction exposes a second failure mode: curriculum targeting induces intra-category sample correlation that inflates effective variance far beyond what $\text{SE}_\text{diff}$ captures under independence assumptions. When the model learns to handle recursive data structures, it improves on all recursive data structure problems simultaneously, introducing positive within-category correlation that standard two-proportion tests ignore.

Let $X_i$ denote a Bernoulli trial representing success on problem $i$ with probability $p_i$. Curriculum targeting clusters these trials into discrete categories $C_k$, each containing $m_k$ problems exhibiting intra-category correlation $\rho_k$. The complete variance expression is:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} p_i(1-p_i) + 2\sum_{i < j} \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j)$$ $$= p(1-p) \cdot \left[n + \sum_k \rho_k \cdot m_k(m_k - 1)\right]$$

For a targeted block of $m = 50$ problems with $\rho = 0.3$ and $p = 0.5$:

$$\text{Var}(X_\text{block}) = 0.25 \cdot [50 + 0.3 \cdot 50 \cdot 49] = 0.25 \cdot 785 = 196.25$$

This yields $\sigma_\text{block} = 14.0$ problems, against the independence assumption value $\sigma_\text{indep} = \sqrt{12.5} \approx 3.54$ problems. The Design Effect is:

$$\text{DEFF} = 1 + (m-1)\rho = 1 + 49 \times 0.3 = 15.7$$

Power Collapse at $n = 2{,}000$

The effective sample size after DEFF correction:

$$n_\text{eff} = \frac{n}{\text{DEFF}} = \frac{2{,}000}{15.7} \approx 127$$

Power recalculation at $n_\text{eff} = 127$, $\delta = 5$ pp, $\alpha = 0.05$ two-sided:

$$\text{SE}_\text{diff} = \sqrt{\frac{2\,p(1-p)}{n_\text{eff}}} = \sqrt{\frac{0.5}{127}} \approx 6.28 \text{ pp}$$ $$z = \frac{\delta}{\text{SE}_\text{diff}} = \frac{5}{6.28} = 0.796$$ $$\text{Power} = \Phi(0.796 - 1.96) = \Phi(-1.164) \approx 12.2\%$$
The DEFF Collapse

Under the independence assumption, $n = 2{,}000$ problems provides 88.5% power to detect a 5 pp improvement at $\alpha = 0.05$. After applying the intra-category correlation correction at $\rho = 0.3$, actual power is 12.2%: nearly indistinguishable from the uncorrected $n = 538$ case. The additional 1,462 problems provide almost no statistical benefit because the curriculum structure introduces correlated sampling that the power calculation ignores.

The covariance addition for the targeted block is $\rho \cdot m(m-1) \cdot p(1-p) = 0.3 \times 2{,}450 \times 0.25 = 183.75$, against an independence contribution of $m \cdot p(1-p) = 12.5$. The covariance term is 14.7× the independence term. The Poisson-Binomial variance reduction from problem heterogeneity (real, in the direction of reducing variance below the binomial upper bound) is completely overwhelmed by this intra-category covariance inflation. The net correction direction is unknown without empirical $\rho$ estimates obtained by running the base model on the evaluation suite twice under different random seeds and computing within-category pass correlation. That measurement is a prerequisite for any valid power calculation; it must precede the generation of a single curriculum problem.

5. Mechanistic Verification and the Curry-Howard Gap

Structural

The Python interpreter provides a binary oracle on output correctness. It cannot distinguish between two mechanistically distinct computation paths that both satisfy the test suite:

Path A (Systemic Reasoning): the network sequentially builds the underlying algorithm, constructing generalizable symbolic logical representations across intermediate layers. Correct output is produced by actually executing the relevant computation.

Path B (Template Matching): the network maps prompt surface features to an internal high-dimensional template, performing abstract pattern completion without generalizable algorithmic synthesis. Correct output is produced on the specific test cases by instantiating a pre-training template, not by constructing the algorithmic solution.

These paths are observationally equivalent under test-suite evaluation and mechanistically distinguishable. The curriculum generation loop compounds this problem: the generator model produces problems that are textually similar to the failure category as it appears in its training distribution, without access to the student model's internal failure mechanism. Some generated problems genuinely test the missing computation; others are solvable by template-matching to patterns the model already knows from pre-training, without requiring the reasoning step that caused the original failure. The oracle passes both. Two diagnostic protocols resolve this ambiguity.

The LogitLens Hooking Protocol

For a 28-layer transformer model, forward linear hooks are registered post-normalization at each intermediate layer to project residual stream states directly into vocabulary space. This surfaces whether correct token probability accretes progressively through the MLP chain (reasoning) or concentrates in the final output layers (template completion):

def register_logit_lens(model):
    layer_logits = []
    for idx, layer in enumerate(model.model.layers):
        def hook_fn(module, input, output, l_idx=idx):
            # Shape: (batch, seq_len, d_model)
            hidden_states = output[0]
            normed_states = model.model.norm(hidden_states)
            # Shape: (batch, seq_len, vocab_size)
            logits = model.lm_head(normed_states)
            layer_logits.append((l_idx, logits.detach().cpu()))
        layer.register_forward_hook(hook_fn)
    return layer_logits

Evaluating the layer-wise token probability $P(\text{target\_token} \mid \text{Layer}_i)$ across all 28 layers yields two diagnostic signatures:

Reasoning signature: target token confidence builds monotonically from mid-network layers, reaching 40–60% of final value by layer 14 (50% depth). This pattern indicates progressive, structured computation distributed across the transformer's MLP chain.

Template signature: target token confidence remains near uniform through layers 1–23, followed by a sharp non-linear jump confined to the output layers (24–28). This exposes late-layer pattern completion without mid-network algorithmic construction: the LM head is doing surface matching, not the MLP chain.

A pre/post fine-tuning comparison on identical held-out problems isolates whether curriculum training shifts internal computation toward reasoning or deepens template mode. The diagnostic requires one forward hook per layer and a held-out set of correctly and incorrectly solved problems; it separates two outcomes that behavioral evaluation cannot separate.

Causal Activation Patching Protocol

To locate specialized category circuits, select $k = 30$ isomorphic problem pairs $(A, B)$ sharing structural properties where the model successfully solves $A$ but fails on $B$, both within the same human-assigned category. For each attention head $h$ at layer $l$, the activation tensor $\mathbf{A}_{h,l}$ is captured during the forward pass of problem $A$ and substituted into the forward pass of problem $B$. The Intervention Effect is:

$$\text{IE}_{h,l} = \mathbb{E}_{\text{pairs}}\left[P\!\left(\text{correct} \mid B,\; \text{patch}_{A \to B}(h,l)\right) - P\!\left(\text{correct} \mid B,\; \text{unpatched}\right)\right]$$

If high $\text{IE}_{h,l}$ concentrates statistically within a bounded set of attention heads (typically 3–5 heads across specific layers), those heads constitute a generalized category-reasoning circuit mapping structural concepts rather than localized prompt formatting. A cross-category check patches activations from a correctly solved dynamic programming problem $A$ into a structurally isomorphic graph traversal problem $B$: if the same heads produce positive IE across categories, the circuit implements general algorithmic reasoning rather than DP-specific memorization.

Cross-Language Transfer Check

Neither the LogitLens nor the patching protocol directly tests cross-domain generalization. That requires generating isomorphic problem pairs in a second language: for each Python curriculum target category, produce idiomatic Rust problems with identical algorithmic structure and input/output contracts. Compute $\delta_\text{Python} = p'_\text{Python} - p_\text{Python}$ and $\delta_\text{Rust} = p'_\text{Rust} - p_\text{Rust}$ after curriculum training.

$\delta_\text{Rust}/\delta_\text{Python} > 0.5$ indicates structural generalization: the curriculum taught an abstract algorithm, not a Python idiom. $\delta_\text{Rust}/\delta_\text{Python} \approx 0$ indicates syntactic template extension. $\delta_\text{Rust} < 0$ indicates severe template overfitting: fine-tuning degraded Rust performance while improving Python performance, a categorical failure of the curriculum's generalization claim.

6. Phase 3 Oracle Fidelity vs. Simulation Speed

Engineering

For Phase 3 (molecular discovery), the DFT latency objection is accurate about the constraint and misidentifies the operative failure mode. Raw DFT on drug-sized molecules runs 8–72 hours per query; synchronous DFT inside a live generation loop is not a viable architecture. Production materials science ML systems do not use it. The standard architecture employs machine learning interatomic potentials (MLIPs): neural networks trained on DFT computations that evaluate molecular energy and forces at approximately $10^5\times$ the speed of the underlying DFT code. CHGNet (Deng et al., 2023) and MatterSim (Microsoft, 2024) are two recent examples. A curriculum loop using an MLIP oracle runs at millisecond latency per evaluation.

The operative failure mode for Phase 3 is distinct: curriculum engines push toward the edges of the current model's capability distribution. In the molecular domain, that means the generator produces candidate structures in regions where the model currently fails, which are precisely the regions where MLIPs are least accurate, because MLIPs are trained on DFT data from chemically reasonable structures and extrapolate poorly to unusual geometries, exotic bonding environments, and unexplored coordination chemistries. The curriculum learns to produce structures with low MLIP-predicted energy not because they are genuinely stable, but because they exploit coverage gaps in the MLIP's training manifold.

Active Learning as a Partial Response

The standard mitigation for MLIP distributional failure is uncertainty-aware active learning: run the curriculum loop against the MLIP, compute epistemic uncertainty via ensemble disagreement across a committee of independently initialized MLIP instances, and route high-uncertainty structures to DFT validation rather than trusting the MLIP score. FLARE (Vandermause et al., 2020), MACE-MP-0 (Batatia et al., 2023), and SevenNet (Park et al., 2024) implement this. A committee of five MACE-MP-0 instances with an uncertainty gate at $\sigma_\text{committee} > 0.05$ eV/atom triggering async DFT validation via PySCF (open-source, Kaggle-deployable) provides a fully specified oracle architecture.

This partially addresses oracle gaming by flagging structures in poorly-covered regions. It does not address interpolation errors: structures where the MACE model is confident but systematically wrong in a pocket of chemical space that happens to fall within the training manifold at a bonding geometry with subtle errors. Whether a sufficiently adversarial curriculum generator finds such pockets depends on MACE-MP-0's specific coverage landscape. The mitigation is to verify that generated structures cluster within the known training manifold via SOAP descriptor distance rather than at its periphery, before accepting MLIP predictions as ground truth. The Phase 3 oracle architecture requires explicit specification of: the MLIP version, the uncertainty metric and threshold triggering DFT fallback, the DFT code and level of theory, and the mechanism by which DFT-validated signal flows back to the curriculum generator.

7. The Failure Taxonomy Problem

Methodological

The curriculum loop requires categorizing failures to generate a targeted curriculum. Human-assigned problem categories (HumanEval labels by algorithm type: sorting, dynamic programming, string manipulation, graph traversal) may not correspond to the model's internal failure modes. A model might fail on graph traversal problems not because it lacks graph reasoning but because it consistently makes off-by-one errors in adjacency list indexing. Both failures surface as "graph traversal" in the human taxonomy but require different curriculum responses.

The inverse is also real. Two problems in different human categories might share a single neural failure mode: failing at pointer arithmetic in C-style simulations and failing at matrix indexing could share a representational deficit in how the model tracks positional state. A curriculum treating them as separate categories generates redundant training signal and misses the underlying deficit.

The correct taxonomy is model-architecture-dependent. The same surface category can involve different internal computations across model families because attention patterns, positional encoding schemes, and MLP specialization differ by architecture. There is no static problem taxonomy simultaneously valid for Qwen 2.5, Llama 3, and Mistral.

Mechanistic Taxonomy Discovery

Before generating any curriculum, run the base model on 500+ evaluation problems with LogitLens hooks active. For each problem, extract the $(28 \times \text{tokens})$ matrix of $P(\text{correct\_token} \mid \text{layer}_i)$. Cluster these trajectories via K-means (K = 8 to 15) on the flattened layer-token-probability vector. Compute the Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) between mechanistic clusters and human-assigned HumanEval categories. If ARI $< 0.3$, the human taxonomy is a poor proxy for model-internal failure modes and must be replaced by the mechanistic taxonomy as the curriculum target. If ARI $> 0.4$, human categories are usable as an approximation. This measurement determines whether the problem with a "dynamic programming" curriculum is that the model lacks DP reasoning, or that it fails at boundary condition tracking, a failure mode that spans multiple human categories and requires a different training signal entirely.

8. Pre-Registration and Contamination Remediation

Testing aggregate pass rate mixes curriculum-targeted and non-targeted problems, conflating signal with noise. VGCL does not claim to improve average pass rate uniformly; it claims to improve pass rate on specific categories it targeted. The experimental design must isolate this claim into a formal multi-hypothesis pre-registration framework.

Multi-Hypothesis Structural Framework

H1 (Targeted Category Improvement): a directional, paired two-sample proportion test executed strictly over problems within curriculum-targeted categories. Pairing over identical problem sets minimizes the effective Design Effect, partially mitigating variance inflation from the DEFF calculation in Section 4. Each targeted category is tested separately with a Bonferroni correction across hypotheses.

H2 (Cross-Language Capability Transfer): an isomorphic generalization test measuring whether curriculum training on a targeted category in Python predicts statistically significant improvement on structural clones translated into Rust. This separates abstract logical acquisition from syntax pattern matching. The ratio $\delta_\text{Rust}/\delta_\text{Python}$ is the primary interpretive quantity: values above 0.5 indicate the curriculum taught structural reasoning; values near 0 indicate template extension; negative values indicate catastrophic overfitting to Python surface patterns.

H3 (Global Bleed-Through Control): a non-inferiority check on non-targeted categories. Curriculum training should produce a strict null result on untargeted problems, confirming the improvement is category-specific rather than a global uncalibrated representation shift from fine-tuning. A statistically significant improvement on non-targeted problems is itself a failure mode, not a success.

Deterministic Benchmark Composition

To achieve a clean evaluation suite of $n = 2{,}000$ distinct problems without introducing evaluation bias or temporal contamination, the benchmark ensemble is locked to the following deterministic composition:

Benchmark Composition (2,000 Total)

$$[\text{HumanEval (164)}] + [\text{MBPP-Sanitized (257)}] + [\text{HumanEval+ v0.1.6 (164)}]$$

$$+ [\text{LiveCodeBench Fixed Window (400)}] + [\text{APPS Introductory (900)}] + [\text{CodeContests Easy (115)}] = 2{,}000$$

MBPP-Sanitized (257 problems) is declared as the canonical variant; the detection threshold at $n = 421$ is 6.89 pp. The LiveCodeBench window is permanently frozen to January 1–June 30, 2024, with problem IDs committed to version control prior to the generation of any curriculum data. MinHash Locality-Sensitive Hashing (LSH) deduplication at a strict 0.85 similarity threshold is enforced across all training-evaluation pairs before the first fine-tuning step. Any generated curriculum problem exceeding this threshold against an evaluation problem is rejected and regenerated.

The effect size prior underpinning the power calculation (specified in Section 4 as 2–5 pp) must be sourced from published curriculum fine-tuning results at comparable scale and compute, specifically CodeRL (Le et al., 2022), STaR (Zeiler et al., 2022), and Self-Play Fine-Tuning studies at 1–2B scale on HumanEval, where the empirical literature range is 5–15 pp at comparable compute. An unsourced effect size prior renders the entire power analysis conditional on an assumption rather than evidence, which invalidates the sample size recommendation.

Empirical ρ Estimation Requirement

The DEFF correction in Section 4 assumes $\rho = 0.3$ as a conservative estimate. The pre-registration document must replace this with an empirically measured value: run the base model on the full 2,000-problem evaluation suite twice under different random seeds and compute within-category pass correlation as Pearson's $\phi$ statistic. This establishes the true intra-category correlation structure for this model family on this benchmark, grounding the DEFF and power calculations in data rather than assumption. The power calculation must be re-run with the empirical $\rho$ before any curriculum generation begins.

9. Training FLOP Efficiency vs. Inference Scaling

Evaluating the absolute value of curriculum training requires a structural boundary distinction that the standard FLOP comparison elides: training compute is a fixed, amortizable overhead; inference compute scales as a linear function of production query volume. These are incommensurable at $T = 1$ query. The operative engineering question is the break-even query volume $T^*$, defined as the point where the fixed compute cost of curriculum training equals the cumulative variable compute cost of extended inference exploration over the base model.

Let $C_\text{train} = 2.7 \times 10^{15}$ FLOPs (Phase 0 curriculum training: 300K tokens at 3 epochs over a 1.5B model) and $C_\text{query} = 2 \times 1.5 \times 10^9 \times 400 \approx 1.2 \times 10^{12}$ FLOPs per single query at 1.5B scale. Total FLOPs over $T$ queries:

Break-Even Query Volume

VGCL approach (fixed training + standard inference): $C_\text{train} + T \cdot C_\text{query}$

Pass@100 approach (repeated sampling, $k = 100$): $T \cdot k \cdot C_\text{query}$

Setting equal and solving for $T$:

$$T^* = \frac{C_\text{train}}{(k-1)\cdot C_\text{query}} = \frac{2.7 \times 10^{15}}{99 \times 1.2 \times 10^{12}} \approx 23 \text{ queries}$$

After 23 queries, curriculum training has strictly lower cumulative FLOP cost than pass@100 sampling. The 7.3× inference-to-training ratio is computed at exactly $T = 1$ and represents the worst possible framing for VGCL amortization.

The per-query FLOP efficiency comparison nonetheless favors test-time compute: pass@100 delivers approximately 30 pp improvement per query at a cost of $k \cdot C_\text{query}$ per query; curriculum training delivers a permanent 5 pp improvement at a one-time cost amortized over $T$. For any finite $T$, pass@100 achieves higher FLOP-per-accuracy-point per query. A model scoring 50% with greedy decoding reaches 80%+ with repeated sampling and test-time verification; the o1 and o3 model series formalize this pattern by allocating inference compute to extended chain-of-thought and internal verification rather than a single greedy pass.

The correct cost-justification frame for VGCL is not efficiency, but capability frontier. For problem classes where $p_\text{base} = 0$, test-time compute delivers $P(\text{solve} \mid k \text{ samples}) = 1 - (1 - p_\text{base})^k = 0$ for all $k$, regardless of sampling budget. Curriculum training can change $p_\text{base}$ from 0 to $\delta > 0$ if the curriculum successfully teaches the required computation. This is where test-time compute and curriculum training are not substitutes but complements on the capability axis.

Decision Rule

Deploy curriculum training when: (a) the target problem class has $p_\text{base} < 0.05$ under greedy and sampling decoding; (b) the failure mode is learnable given the pre-training gravity radius at the chosen model scale, verified by the LogitLens reasoning signature; and (c) expected query volume $T > 100$ over the deployment lifetime. Any cost justification for curriculum training is analytically incomplete without explicitly computing $T^*$ and verifying condition (a). Outside these parameters, compute is more efficiently allocated at inference time.
